ARE AUCTION HOUSES GENDER BIASED?

Is there really a difference between male artists and female artists? What about the top five auction houses? How do they behave in an auction world predominantly dominated by men?

USED DATA: the relative difference between the hammer price and the estimated price per auction house.
Firstly, the difference between hammer price and the average estimated price for each piece of art was calculated. Then it was calculated how much this difference was in percentage of the hammer price, which resulted in a relative difference. Then, the median of all works sold in a specific auction house was calculated because the number of artworks in the auction houses are not the same.

In the graph below the distribution of the median of the relative differences of artworks by men and women for all the action houses are visualized.

From the graph it can be concluded with a high level of confidentiality that men generally have a more consistent price setting, as the median of the relative differences is constant. Prices remain much more around the zero-line compared to the data for the female artworks. For female artworks, the relative differences are slightly higher, which suggests that the pricing of female works is less stable in comparison to male artworks. A possible conclusion might be that buyers often tend to bid higher for male works than for female works.

The following graph visualizes the gender biased appreciation of the top 5 auction houses (Christie's, Sotheby's, Philips, Philips de Pury & Compant, and Bonhams).

It can be concluded that Sotheby's and Christie's estimate their prices differently. Christie’s and Sotheby's both make a profit on the estimated price for male and female art. At Christies, women get a higher hammer price in percentage than their male colleagues. Sotheby's is different, and more emancipatory, as there is hardly any difference between the hammer price and the estimated price for both genders.
For Philips de Pury & Company, it can be said that the male artworks are perfectly estimated, but female artworks are overestimated.
At Philips (before 2004) female artworks were also overestimated and male works underestimated. Bonhams overestimates the artworks of both genders, but the difference is higher for female artwork (in the negative percentage).


CONCLUSION

Conclusion: Whether auction houses are biased is auction house dependent. It can be concluded from this that the duopoly Sotheby's and Christie's estimate their prices differently. At Christie’s, women get a relative higher hammer price than their male colleagues. At Sotheby's, gains are always made on the hammer price for both genders, but the difference between the male and female data is hardly noticeable.
